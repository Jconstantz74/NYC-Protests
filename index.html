Brooklyn Rises Up

Here’s what happened during a summer of protests.


By Jo Constantz | Oct. 14, 2020




Black Lives Matter protests have gripped the nation since George Floyd was killed by police in May. The Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project (ACLED) collected data on the protests for its U.S. Crisis Monitor project, an initiative that tracks unrest across the country with the aim of preventing and mitigating the risk of political violence and instability going into the 2020 presidential election.


The data shows that while more than 93% of BLM protests across the country are peaceful, state intervention and use of force has increased. Police or other authorities intervene in nearly one in 10 events, and “of these interventions, government personnel used force in more than half –– at least 54%.”


In Brooklyn, 34 protests were documented in all. Here’s a look at what happened this summer, according to the data.

A woman was pushed to the ground by an officer. Some rioters vandalized NYPD cars, including setting vehicles on fire. Multiple officers were injured.

According to ACLED data, about 91% of protests in Brooklyn over the summer were nonviolent. While demonstrations in the first few days after Floyd’s death turned violent, protests over the following months were largely peaceful.

About the data
In its data, ACLED codes demonstrators as either “rioters” or “protesters.” In its FAQs, ACLED says this coding is a methodological distinction, rather than a normative one. “‘Protesters’ refers to demonstrators that are peaceful, and not engaging in any destructive or disruptive behavior (e.g. violence, vandalism, looting, etc.) ‘Rioters,’ on the other hand, refer to demonstrators engaging in violence, vandalism, looting, etc. If the character of an event changes from non-violent to violent, then the event will be coded as how it ultimately ended (i.e., violently).”

However, “it is important to note that an event noting ‘Rioters’ does not indicate that every single demonstrator engaged in violence.”

It is also important to note that multiple events may be recorded for the same day. “If two events occur concurrently in the same or a similar location, and it is clear that they are distinct, independent, and differ with respect to peaceful protest or rioting, two events are coded.”
